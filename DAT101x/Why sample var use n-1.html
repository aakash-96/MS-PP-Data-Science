The key of understanding lies in fundamentals of mathematics/geometry. Let’s put away all the equations for a moment. Imagine any, let’s say 2-dimensional object - for example square. All the points on the edge (black & red), which form our square, represent population. The red points on the edge, represent our sample.

Mean is a measure of central tendency and it is represented by “middle value”. In math/geometry mean is represented by the center of the object. So, red point inside the square represent the sample mean, black point inside the square represent population mean ( or at least it should look like that :) )

Now, let’s think about it for a second. You will find out, that the distance between the red insider and all the red outsiders on the edge, is the smallest possible distance you can measure. There is no other point inside the square (or anywhere else), which is closer to ALL the red points, than this one (in other words, the sum of distances is the smallest possible number). Of course, the same applies for the population centre.

Now, let’s measure “the average distance” between the centre and the outsiders. Distance 1 + Distance 2 + Distance 3 + Distance 4, divided by 4, since we have four samples. In some sense, we’ve just measured variance (in other words, if all the edge points should be equally distributed around the center, while the total distance measured remains the same, how would the picture look like ? ). You can measure both, sample variance & population variance. Here, “n-1” takes the place in the story. In sample variance, we are measuring average distance between the Red center & red outsiders. We don’t know that population look like square, we see only the red points!!! In a perfect world, we would know that population forms a square, and subsequently we would know, where the population mean/center is. World unfortunately, is not a perfect place. But we can try to make it as perfect as possible (that’s why we use “n-1).

We are using statistics, because we want to ESTIMATE the truth, the population. Sample is just a tool how to estimate it. What i am trying to say is, that we are measuring distance between the sample and it’s center, but we are actually interested in the measurement of distance between the sample (which is the subset of the population) and the POPULATION CENTER. Sample mid-point is not the part of the truth, but since we don’t know the true mid-point, we have to use the sample mid point. To eliminate this imperfection, we implement “n-1”.

As we already know, the red-centre represents the smallest possible distance between the centre and all the other red points . If you will measure the average distance between the sample red points and the true-black mid point, you will always, always get a bigger number. That’s why we are using “n-1” in denominator, so we can get a little bit bigger number as a result, which is closer to truth. Statistics tests shown us, that if we would use “n-2”, we would already overestimate the truth. By using just “n” we are underestimating the truth. That’s why we use “n-1”.

Makes sense ? I hope yes. I highly recommend to watch some khan academy videos on this problem.

Boyi Guo, M.S. Statistics & Biostatistics, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign

Two good answers from Parth Dave and Guy Liron, about the concept of the sample variance being unbiased. A related question is, why do we use the term “n−1n−1 degrees of freedom” for this estimator? (This is just intended to be a first step toward understanding that concept; it’s deep enough that it takes a fair amount of study and thought.)

Suppose I tell you I have 10 observations x1,x2,…,x10x1,x2,…,x10, with a mean of (say) 12. Can you give me a list of 10 numbers which has that same mean?

That’s a pretty wide-open question, and it turns out it has a pretty wide-open answer. That is, you can make 9 completely arbitrary choices (9 “degrees of freedom”), but then your 10th choice has been made for you.

For example, you could start with nine 00's for x1x1 through x9x9. But then x10x10 must be 120 (in order for the mean to be 12). Or you could choose xi=ixi=i for 1≤i≤91≤i≤9. In that case, x10x10 would have to be 75. You could even choose xi=10100xi=10100 (a googol) for 1≤i≤91≤i≤9, as long as you choose x10x10 to be 120 minus 9 googol.

Put another way: ∑i=1n−1(xi−x¯¯¯)∑i=1n−1(xi−x¯) could add up to anything, but ∑i=1n(xi−x¯¯¯)∑i=1n(xi−x¯) must add up to 0.

The sample variance is essentially the average of (x1−x¯¯¯)2,(x2−x¯¯¯)2,…,(xn−x¯¯¯)2(x1−x¯)2,(x2−x¯)2,…,(xn−x¯)2, but we divide by n−1n−1 instead of nn as a way of recognizing that the list (x1−x¯¯¯),(x2−x¯¯¯),…,(xn−x¯¯¯)(x1−x¯),(x2−x¯),…,(xn−x¯) really only contains n−1n−1 “free” values.

Finally, I note that in the “related questions” list for this page, there are LOTS of other similar questions. One can almost certainly gain more insight by reading some of the answers to those questions.

Answered Jun 15, 2016

To elaborate a little bit on Parth Dave’s short, yet accurate, answer: given NN samples of a random variable xx with mean μ=E[x]μ=E[x] and variance σ2=E[(x−μ)2]σ2=E[(x−μ)2], the unbiased estimator of the mean is

μ^=1N∑Nn=1xnμ^=1N∑n=1Nxn.

Notice that this is an estimator of the mean, based on your sample, hence it has some estimation error. This error has zero-mean (the estimator is unbiased), but it does have positive variance.

Now, let’s estimate the variance. If you know the actual population mean μμ, you can use it, and than the unbiased estimator is

σ^2μ=1N∑Nn=1(xn−μ)2σ^μ2=1N∑n=1N(xn−μ)2.

Usually, you wouldn’t know the population mean, and than you have to use the estimated value μ^μ^. In this case, the error of the estimated mean changes the sum, and adds a term that happens to have a mean value of exactly −σ2−σ2 to the expression (try and calculate, it’s not that difficult), so the unbiased estimator must be altered to remain unbiased, and take the form

σ^2μ^=1N−1∑Nn=1(xn−μ^)2σ^μ^2=1N−1∑n=1N(xn−μ^)2.

Answered Jun 23, 2016
If your data is Normal(\mu, \sigma^2), the sample variance (with n-1) is an unbiased estimator of \sigma^2 . (That is, its expected value is \sigma^2)

The maximum likelihood estimator (with n), is biased, but asymptotically they are pretty much equivalent.
